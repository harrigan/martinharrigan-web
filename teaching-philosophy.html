Teaching Philosophy

Introduction

The subject area of my teaching is software development. I have taught classes of students at third-level at SETU Waterford and SETU Carlow; I have guided individual students at a Teaching & Learning Centre at SETU Waterford and I have participated in volunteer-led programmes such as CoderDojo. I have also published research into the design of integrated learning systems [Harrigan et al. 2009, Harrigan & Wade 2009]. However, I feel a greater degree of uncertainty in my competencies as a teacher than as an expert in my subject area. Therefore, as a teacher, I try to start from a position of knowledge: Software development is a craft and its practitioners are craftspeople [Reeves 1992, Hunt & Thomas 1999, McBreen 2001]. They use specialised tools to produce products that attain some goal in some environment. They take pride in their work and view the process as a reward in itself. In this context, learning is a journey from apprentice to craftsperson, and perhaps, to master. Teaching is that which creates “a stimulating and pervasive craft spirit” [Bagley 2012].

This key belief, that software development is a craft and learners wish to become craftspeople, guides me in my teaching and helps me to structure my teaching philosophy. In the following sections, whose structure is adapted from Chism [Chism 1998] and Nilson [Nilson 2010, pg. 321], I will discuss my assumptions about teaching and learning that underlie this philosophy, the goals it sets for my students, what it implies for my classroom, and my plans for future development.

Teaching & Learning

If learning is a journey from apprentice to crafts-person, then it is a journey with many ups and downs. Learners are rewarded through new experiences, observable progress, fruitful teamwork, and reaching one's destination. Indeed, “one's destination is never a place, but rather a new way of looking at things” [Miller 1957]. However, learners may also experience stagnation, frustration, wrong-turns and detours, and a failure to reach one's destination. In hindsight some of these difficulties may be beneficial. Learning requires perseverance and the ability to tolerate frustration [Wilde 2012]. Detours can be worthwhile and failures can be productive [Kapur 2008]. Even failing to reach one's destination can be a good outcome; the learner may be able to identify a preferred destination.

As a teacher, I am a guide and a motivator for some part of the learner's journey. Having travelled my own path, not necessarily an identical one to theirs, I can assure them of its value. I can identify suitable way-points. The learners may not be able to reach the way-points on their own but they can with assistance from fellow learners and myself [Wass & Golding 2014]. I provide examples of good planning. I encourage the learners to reflect on their progress. I emphasise the positive experiences and ameliorate the bad. However, it is important to note that the learners are making the journeys for themselves.

Implementation of the Philosophy

In practice, teaching involves trade-offs. I can only make a journey as enjoyable as possible subject to constraints such as timetables and the physical location of classrooms.

As a motivator I seek motivational triggers. Sennett [Sennett 2008] argues that motivation “is more important than talent in consummating craftsmanship”. It is insufficient to simply state the importance of the subject matter, or claim it to be a required prerequisite for some future content. While both these claims may be true, they do not create an immediate and selfish desire to engage with the content. In a computer programming module I introduced students to malware. In order to better motivate the topic, I examined a popular news story at the time: Lenovo was installing adware on new computers. The adware was created by Superfish and Komodia and injected advertisements into secure connections by performing man-in-the-middle attacks. The story was difficult to follow due to the technical details. I used this piece of malware as a case study and explained in detail how it functioned and why it was unacceptable for Lenovo to install this software on new computers. The students were motivated by a desire to understand the story but also to protect themselves. Some were owners of Lenovo laptops.

As a guide, I organize lesson plans and assessments that encourage continuous engagement. Best practices such as those described by Nilson [Nilson 2010, pg. 273 and pg. 281] help structure formative and summative assessments. In particular, formative assessment encourages students to engage in a low-stakes environment. In a computer security module I introduced a game called ‘Elevation of Privilege’ [Microsoft 2020] to help the students familiarize themselves with the process of threat modelling. The game is a simple card game that requires students to examine a data flow diagram through a number of different lenses: spoofing, tampering, repudiation, information disclosure, denial of service and elevation of privilege. By considering each of these in turn, the student can discover for themselves possible vulnerabilities in the data flow diagram.

Future Development

Craftsmanship requires practice. However, not all practice makes perfect. Sedano [Sedano 2012] describes deliberate practice to help a learner improve their craft. This includes some novel steps that should work well in the classroom. I intend to experiment with this and similar approaches.

Conclusion

Initially I was sceptical as to the merits of a teaching philosophy. Haggerty [Haggerty 2010] argues that many contain “abstract appeals to unobjectionable ambitions”. However, after writing this I have had a change of mind. This exercise provided me with an opportunity to reflect on my profession and my strategies in the classroom. If my teaching philosophy is drab and predictable then what does that say about my teaching? I am not satisfied with its current state but I believe that is the point: I hope the next version will improve upon it.
